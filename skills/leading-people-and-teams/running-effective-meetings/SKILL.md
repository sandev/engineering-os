# Running Effective Meetings

A meeting spends the most expensive resource you have, many people's attention at once. Treat it that way: most meetings should be shorter, smaller, or an async message instead. The ones worth holding have a clear purpose, the right people, and end with decisions and owners.

## First: does this need to be a meeting?

Meet only when live interaction beats async, for **deciding, aligning, building relationships, or complex real-time discussion.** If the goal is to *share information*, send a doc or message; a status meeting that could be an email steals hours across the org. Default to async; earn the meeting.

## Set it up to be worth the time

- **Purpose + desired outcome** stated in the invite: why we're meeting and what we'll leave with. No purpose, no meeting.
- **An agenda** (even three bullets), shared ahead. Attach a **pre-read** for anything that needs context, and expect people to read it.
- **Only the necessary people.** Every extra attendee raises cost and lowers participation. Optional attendees are optional; invitees should have a reason to be there.
- **Right length**: default to 25/50 minutes, not 30/60; most meetings expand to fill the slot. Shorter forces focus.

## Facilitate

- **Start on time; state the goal** in the first minute.
- **Keep it on track**: park tangents in a visible "parking lot" and return to the agenda.
- **Draw people out.** The loudest voice isn't the best idea; explicitly invite quieter and remote attendees. Watch for the HiPPO effect (see the `decision-making` skill).
- **Drive to a conclusion**, not just discussion. If a decision is needed, name the decision-maker and get the call made.
- **End with decisions, owners, and next steps**: verbally, before anyone leaves.

## Always close the loop

Send brief notes: **decisions made, action items (owner + date), and open questions.** A meeting with no written outcome didn't fully happen, people forget, and those who missed it are lost. Track action items to done.

## Common meeting types (quick reference)

- **All-hands / town hall:** inform and align a whole org, and build culture. Lead with what matters (context, wins, changes, direction), keep updates tight, and protect real time for Q&A (seed questions if needed). It's two-way, not a broadcast, and it's a moment to reinforce strategy (see the `technical-strategy` skill).
- **Decision meeting:** get a specific decision made. Frame the decision, options, and recommendation in a pre-read; use the time to resolve objections and decide, not to present.
- **Kickoff:** align a new project on goals, scope, roles, and risks.
- **Review / demo:** show working results and gather feedback.
- **1:1s and sprint ceremonies** have their own playbooks, see the `feedback-and-1-1s` and `agile-ceremonies` skills.

## Anti-patterns

- A meeting that should have been a message.
- No agenda or desired outcome, a discussion that wanders and decides nothing.
- Inviting everyone "to be safe"; the room is too big to participate.
- The organizer talking the whole time (a broadcast, not a meeting).
- Ending with no decisions, owners, or notes.
- Recurring meetings that never get cancelled even when there's nothing to cover.
- Status meetings that exist only to report what a dashboard or doc already shows.
