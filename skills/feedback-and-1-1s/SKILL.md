# Feedback & One-on-ones

Feedback is a gift that helps someone improve; 1:1s are the recurring space where trust, growth, and small problems get handled before they become big ones. Both fail the same way: avoidance. Vague, delayed, or withheld feedback is the most common failure of engineering leaders — it feels kind and is actually unkind, because it denies people the chance to grow.

## Feedback: be specific, timely, and direct

Care personally **and** challenge directly. Warmth without candor is useless; candor without warmth is cruelty. Aim for both.

Use **SBI** — Situation, Behavior, Impact — to keep feedback factual and non-accusatory:

- **Situation:** when/where it happened (concrete, recent).
- **Behavior:** what the person actually did (observable, not your interpretation).
- **Impact:** the effect it had (on the work, the team, you).

<example>
Vague: "You need to communicate better."
SBI: "In yesterday's review (S), the design doc went out an hour before the
meeting (B), so people came unprepared and we couldn't decide (I). Can we get
it out a day ahead next time?"
</example>

### Principles
- **Timely** — give it close to the event, while it's concrete. Saved-up feedback lands as an ambush.
- **Praise in public, correct in private.** Recognition is as important as correction — catch people doing things right.
- **On behavior, not identity** — "the doc was late," not "you're disorganized." Behavior is changeable; character attacks just wound.
- **End with a clear ask** and offer support. Feedback with no path forward is just criticism.
- **No surprises at review time.** Formal reviews should summarize feedback the person has already heard.

## Receiving feedback

Model what you want: listen without defending, thank the giver, ask for specifics, and act on it visibly. A leader who bristles at feedback won't get any — and will be the last to know they're off track.

## One-on-ones

The 1:1 is **the report's meeting, not your status update.** Its job is their growth, blockers, and the things that don't surface in group settings.

- **Protect it.** Recurring, and rarely cancelled — cancelling signals they don't matter.
- **Let them drive** the agenda; keep a shared running doc so nothing recurs unaddressed.
- **Listen more than you talk.** Ask open questions: what's blocking you? what's frustrating? where do you want to grow?
- **Status is not the point** — get project updates elsewhere; use this time for the human, career, and the unsaid.
- **Track and close loops** on commitments you make; unkept 1:1 promises destroy trust fast.
- Skip-levels and peer 1:1s use the same posture: listen, build trust, surface problems early.

## Anti-patterns

- Avoiding hard feedback to keep the peace — the kindest-seeming, most damaging choice.
- Hoarding feedback for the performance review (surprises).
- Feedback on personality/character instead of behavior.
- The 1:1 as a status meeting the manager runs.
- Cancelling 1:1s whenever things get busy.
- Only ever giving corrective feedback; never recognizing good work.
- Making a commitment in a 1:1 and quietly dropping it.
